The Complete One-Arm Pull-Up Guide

A 3-Phase Plan to Build Toward Your First Strict One-Arm Pull-Up

By CaliCalculator Team • March 2026

The one-arm pull-up is one of the most impressive feats of upper body strength. It demands exceptional pulling power, grip strength, shoulder stability, core control, and tendon resilience. Unlike regular pull-ups, you cannot rely on symmetrical force distribution. Every weakness gets exposed.

This guide breaks the journey into three logical phases:

  • Phase 1: Baseline Strength
  • Phase 2: Assisted & Eccentric Development
  • Phase 3: Specific One-Arm Practice

Warm-Up Routine (Do This Before Every Session)

Never attempt heavy weighted pull-ups or one-arm work cold. Your elbows and shoulders must be prepared.

  • General Warm-Up (5 mins): Jump rope, Arm circles, Band pull-aparts (x20), Scapular push-ups (x15).
  • Scapular Pull-Ups (2×10): Pull shoulder blades down and back. Do NOT bend elbows.
  • Dead Hang (30s): Activate grip and decompress shoulders.
  • Light Pull-Ups (2×5): Smooth, controlled reps.
Phase 1: Baseline Strength

Goal: Build raw pulling strength.

Standard: 5×5 Weighted Pull-Ups with 25–30 kg (55–66 lbs) OR 50% of bodyweight.

Exercise: Weighted Pull-Ups (5×5)

  • "Pull elbows to your ribs."
  • "Chest tall, core tight."
  • "Control the descent (2–3 seconds)."
Phase 2: Assisted & Eccentric Development

Goal: Unilateral development.

Exercise: Assisted One-Arm Pull-Ups (3×10 total reps)

  • Use resistance bands for help.
  • "Keep shoulder packed down."
  • "Minimal rotation."

Exercise: One-Arm Negatives (2 Sets per Arm)

  • 5–8 second descent from chin-over-bar position.
  • "Fight gravity. Slow is strength."
Phase 3: Specific One-Arm Practice

Goal: Maximal neural effort.

Exercise: One-Arm Pull-Up Attempts (2–3 per arm)

  • Rest 2–3 minutes between attempts.
  • Grip hard and engage scapula first.

Exercise: Assisted Chin-Ups (3×5)

  • Palm facing you. Builds critical elbow flexor strength.

Technical Mastery: Form Breakdown

  • Scapular Engagement: Always shoulder depression first.
  • Core Stability: Tight abs prevent excessive twisting.
  • Grip Strength: Crush the bar; weak grip limits output.

Injury Prevention Guide

One-arm pull-up training stresses: Elbows (medial epicondyle), Biceps tendon, Rotator cuff, and Lats.

  • ✔ Train 2–3 times per week max
  • ✔ Add forearm extensor work (reverse curls)
  • ✔ Do light band tricep pushdowns for elbow balance
  • ✔ If pain persists, deload immediately

How Long Does It Take?

If you hit the 5×5 weighted standard and weigh under 90 kg (198 lbs), you may achieve a one-arm pull-up in 4–8 months. Heavier athletes may take longer.

FAQ: One-Arm Pull-Up Training

1. Should I train with a Pull-Up (overhand) or Chin-Up (underhand) grip?

Most athletes find the Chin-Up (supinated) grip easier initially because it allows more bicep recruitment. However, the One-Arm Pull-Up (pronated/neutral) is considered the "true" standard. Training both helps build overall joint resilience.

2. Why does my body spin uncontrollably during the pull?

Spinning is caused by a lack of rotational core stability and lats engagement. To fix this, focus on squeezing your glutes and your non-working arm's fist during the pull to create full-body tension.

3. Can I use a towel or a second hand on the wrist for assistance?

Yes, "Archer Pull-ups" or holding a towel with the non-working hand are valid progressions. However, resistance bands provide more consistent, measurable scaling for Phase 2 and 3.

4. I have "Golfer's Elbow" (inner elbow pain). Should I keep training?

No. One-arm work puts extreme torque on the medial epicondyle. If you feel sharp pain, stop immediately. Focus on "Phase 1" weighted pull-ups or high-volume rows until the connective tissue heals.

5. Does grip width on the weighted pull-up matter for OAP?

Yes. Training weighted pull-ups with a narrower grip (closer to shoulder width) better mimics the line of pull needed for a one-arm execution.

6. How important is body fat percentage for this move?

Extremely important. Because this is a pure relative strength feat, every extra pound of non-functional mass (body fat) exponentially increases the difficulty of the lift.

7. Should I use chalk?

Absolutely. Your grip is the "contact point" for the entire chain of force. If your hand is slipping even slightly, your nervous system will "downregulate" (reduce) the power sent to your lats to protect you from falling.

8. Can I train OAP and Front Lever on the same day?

You can, as they are both pulling movements. However, since both are high-intensity for the tendons, ensure you aren't doing max effort on both in the same session. Alternate which one you prioritize first.
